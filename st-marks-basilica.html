Saint Mark's Basilica

Basilica di San Marco

Timeless Masterpiece

St. Mark's Basilica (Basilica di San Marco) in Venice is one of the most iconic churches in the world, known for its stunning Italo-Byzantine architecture and rich history. Originally built in 829, it was meant to house the stolen relics of Saint Mark, the patron saint of Venice, which were smuggled from Alexandria. The basilica was rebuilt in its current form in 1063 under Doge Domenico I Contarini , reflecting Venice’s growing power and wealth.

Over the centuries, it has been adorned with golden mosaics, intricate marble facades, and treasures looted from Constantinople during the Fourth Crusade (1202-1204), including the famous four bronze horses above the entrance. Once the private chapel of the Doge, it became the city’s cathedral in 1807.

Golden Basilica

It is often called the "Church of Gold" (Chiesa d'Oro) due to its lavish gold mosaics, which cover over 8,000 square meters of the interior.

Hidden Architectural Mix

While it is mainly Byzantine in style, the basilica also features Gothic, Renaissance, and Islamic influences, showcasing Venice’s history as a major trading hub between East and West.

Stolen Relics

The basilica was built to house the relics of Saint Mark, which were allegedly smuggled out of Alexandria, Egypt, in 828 by Venetian merchants.

Art & War

Crusaders & Conquerors

In 1204, during the Fourth Crusade, Venetian-led forces sacked Constantinople and brought back treasures, including the famous Horses of St. Mark and parts of the church’s golden decorations. Venetian merchants and military leaders played a key role in this looting, enriching the basilica.

Architects & Artists

Over the centuries, numerous architects and craftsmen contributed to its unique mix of Byzantine, Gothic, and Renaissance styles. The lavish gold mosaics were designed by Venetian and Greek artists, and some were even later restored by Titian and Tintoretto, two of Venice’s greatest painters.

Napoleon Bonaparte

In 1797, Napoleon invaded Venice, abolished the Venetian Republic, and looted many of basilica's treasures to be melted dwon, parts of which were later returned. In 1807, he made St. Mark’s the official cathedral of Venice, ending its status as the Doge’s private chapel.

Architectural Marvels & Floor Guide

Ground Floor Over 8,000 sq. meters of golden mosaics adorn the walls and domes, while the Pala d’Oro dazzles with 1,900 gemstones. The marble floors feature intricate geometric and animal patterns, and the iconic bronze horses above the entrance are replicas, with the originals displayed upstairs.

First Floor The original bronze horses, looted from Constantinople in 1204, are preserved inside for protection. The Basilica Museum showcases ancient mosaics, tapestries, and relics, while the panoramic terrace offers stunning views of Piazza San Marco and the Venetian lagoon.

Second Floor Some upper areas feature private chapels and additional mosaics, though they are not always open to the public. They offer a unique, close-up view of the domes and intricate mosaics.

Accessibility

People with physical disabilities and wheelchair users are able to enter the Basilica and also gain access to the Treasury through the Porta dei Fiori , which is equipped with a ramp. The Museum, which is located on the upper floor of the Basilica, is served by both a lift and stair lifts. Visitors are kindly requested to contact a staff member at the Porta dei Fiori, which is located on the left-hand side of the Basilica.
