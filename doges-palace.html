Doge's Palace

Palazzo Ducale

A Fortress of Political Might

The Doge’s Palace (Palazzo Ducale) is the embodiment of Venice’s power, both political and economic. The stunning architecture reflects the wealth and influence the Venetian Republic once commanded across the Mediterranean.

For centuries, it was the seat of the Doge, the elected ruler of Venice, and the home of the Venetian government. The Doge, who was chosen by an electoral system, held immense power, but was also constrained by the many councils that governed the Republic.

It also housed the council chambers, the court of justice, and even the prisons for those who opposed the Republic. These prisons were located in the lower floors of the palace.

Marino Faliero (1274-1355)

The only Doge executed for treason—his portrait was erased from the Sala del Maggior Consiglio (Hall of the Great Council) in shame.

Francesco Foscari (1373-1457)

The 65th Doge had the longest reign, lasting 34 years, 6 months and 8 days. The death of his last son caused him to withdraw from his duties and was soon forced to abdicate. His death only a week later provoked a massive pblic outcry and he was given a state funeral

Enrico Dandolo (1192-1205)

The blind but ruthless Doge who orchestrated the sack of Constantinople during the Fourth Crusade. His bold strategies expanded Venice’s influence but left a legacy of destruction. One of the many items he sent back to Venice are the four Horses of St. Mark that decorate the cathedral to this day.

Prisoners & Myths

Casanova's Great Escape (1756)

The legendary seducer Giacomo Casanova was imprisoned in the attic-like Piombi prison, but he escaped by digging through the floor with a metal spike!

The Bridge of Sighs

Prisoners took their last look at Venice through its windows before their fate was sealed. Some say you can still hear their sighs if you listen carefully at dusk. The bridge’s name is said to come from the mournful sighs of the prisoners as they crossed it on their way to the prison, knowing they will never leave.

The Ghosts of the Doge's Palace

Some believe that the Doge’s Palace is haunted by the spirits of prisoners who never made it out.

Architectural Marvels & Floor Guide

Must see architecture

The Scala d’Oro (Golden Staircase) is an extravagant grand entrance reserved for the most powerful visitors. La Scala d'Oro was one of the last major works made in the Doge's Palace during the 16th century

A Floating Masterpiece, the Doge’s Palace sits on wooden piles submerged in the lagoon – these foundations have kept the palace stable for over 500 years! They can be seen from the exterior near the palace's waterfront facade.

Secret Passages and Hidden Doors allowed rulers to spy on their subjects and move around unseen. These secret routes can be explored as part of the museum tour

The Courtyard features the wide Censors’ Staircase leading inside the palace and the Giants’ Staircase, guarded by Sansovino’s two colossal statues of Mars and Neptune, representing Venice’s power by land and by sea.

The State Rooms are gigantic halls with massive golden ceilings, including the Chamber of the Great Council, which once the largest room in Europe!

The Piombi prison is located on the upper floors of the palace. It is accessible via the Bridge of Sighs, offering a glimpse into the harsh conditions faced by prisoners.

Accssibility Informtation

Regular visit is fully accessible. The restrooms on the ground floor are fully accessible. However, The Secret Itineraries, Prisons and Armoury are not accessible for wheelchairs.
