Marciana Library

Biblioteca Nazionale Marciana

Venice's Treasure of Knowledge

The Marciana Library (Biblioteca Nazionale Marciana) in Venice is one of the oldest and most important libraries in Italy, known for its vast collection of classical texts and stunning Renaissance architecture. Founded in the 16th century, it was envisioned as a grand repository for knowledge, fulfilling the wishes of Cardinal Bessarion, who donated his vast collection of Greek and Latin manuscripts to Venice in 1468. Construction began in 1537 under the renowned architect Jacopo Sansovino, and the library became a masterpiece of Venetian Renaissance design.

Located in Piazza San Marco, near the Doge’s Palace, it houses rare manuscripts, ancient maps, and works by famous scholars. Today, the Marciana Library remains a symbol of Venetian scholarship and artistic achievement.

A Gift of Knowledge

The library was founded after Cardinal Bessarion, a Greek scholar and former Archbishop of Constantinople, donated over 1,000 rare Greek and Latin manuscripts to Venice in 1468 to preserve classical knowledge.

Designed by a Renaissance Master

The library’s stunning architecture was created by Jacopo Sansovino, one of the greatest Renaissance architects, who also designed parts of St. Mark’s Square.

Home to Rare Treasures

Among its priceless collections, the library holds a 15th-century edition of Ptolemy’s "Geography," early printed books, and the world’s oldest surviving complete manuscript of Homer’s "Iliad."

Notable Figures

Titian

The renowned Venetian painter Titian contributed to the library’s decoration, creating some of the ceiling frescoes.

Galileo Galilei

Galileo gave public lectures at here in the early 1600s, he demonstrated his telescope and presented his astronomical discoveries.

Tomaso Giannotti

Appointed in 1560, he became the first official librarian of the Marciana Library and helped organize its growing collection.

Elisabetta Caminer Turra

A pioneering Venetian female writer and publisher, Turra used the Marciana Library for research and is considered one of the first professional female journalists in Italy.

Architectural Marvels & Floor Guide

Ground Floor Admire the elegant 16th-century Renaissance-style façade designed by Jacopo Sansovino, featuring marble sculptures, including works by Alessandro Vittoria, at the entrance. Inside, a bookshop offering historical books, guides, and souvenirs

First Floor Sala Sansoviniana features frescoes by Titian, Veronese, and Tintoretto, with a ceiling depicting ancient philosophers and gods. It also houses 16th-century globes, rare manuscripts, and ancient Greek and Latin texts.

Second Floor The Scholars' Reading Room offers access to original manuscripts and rare books, while the Historical Archives hold Venice's state documents, maps, and scientific texts. It also features Ptolemy’s Geography, one of the first modern atlases.

Accessibility

The ground and first floors are generally accessible, but upper levels lack elevators and have narrow stairs, limiting access to reading rooms and archives. Visitors needing help should check in at the information desk. For a more comfortable visit, arriving early is recommended. The library lacks accessible restrooms; the nearest ones are in Piazza San Marco (fee required).
